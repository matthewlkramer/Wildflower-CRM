1. Freshest UI direction (what "modern" reads as in 2026)
The patterns actually shipping in the most-admired products (Linear, Notion, Stripe, Attio, Vercel):

Calm design / progressive disclosure. Default views show only what the immediate task needs; power features exist but stay hidden until invoked. "Feels like Linear" is the compliment everyone chases — whitespace as a functional tool, typography doing the hierarchy work instead of icons and borders.
AI as invisible infrastructure, not a badge. The "✨ AI" branding is now considered dated. Winners embed intelligence inline — auto-classification on save, contextual suggestions in the field you're editing — rather than a separate AI panel. We're actually well-positioned here: email intelligence, next-step suggestions, and donor auto-matching already follow the review-queue model; the trend says surface them more inline and brand them less.
Keyboard-first + command palette. Cmd-K as the universal entry point for navigation and actions, not just search.
Data-dense, but with one narrative. The anti-pattern everyone calls out is the raw log/wall-of-fields. The fix is "progressive summarization": a derived current-status line at the top, full detail one click below.
2. How QB-challengers do reconciliation (Puzzle, Digits, Ramp, Mercury + AI recon platforms)
This stream converged on a remarkably consistent playbook:

Continuous reconciliation, not month-end. Transactions reconcile as they arrive; the UI is a daily inbox, not a periodic project. Puzzle's whole pitch vs QB is "no month-end panic."
Two-layer matching with confidence scores. Deterministic rules clear 60–70% automatically; fuzzy/AI matching proposes the rest with a visible confidence level. High-confidence matches are one-click-confirm (or auto-confirm above a threshold); only genuine exceptions demand human attention.
The inbox pattern (Ramp is the reference). One badge-counted inbox with an explicit state machine — items only enter the queue when they're actually actionable (cleared + complete); everything else stays out. Four states: all / needs-review / flagged / done. The insight: ruthlessly filter what's allowed to claim attention.
Exceptions never block the pipeline. Unmatched items route to review while everything else flows through.
Relevance to us: this validates the direction of the ratified reconciliation redesign (two clear reports replacing six queues). The gaps worth stealing: (a) we don't show match confidence on donor/gift auto-matches — a score with one-click confirm for high-confidence rows would cut review time substantially; (b) an entry gate so rows only appear in the workbench when truly actionable, with a badge count; (c) "percent auto-cleared" as a visible health metric.

3. How Salesforce-challengers show customer records (Attio, Twenty, Folk)
Attio's record-page anatomy is the current benchmark: a highlights strip (up to 6 key attributes as widgets at the top — the KPI strip for a person), action buttons, then a unified timeline of every email, meeting, note, task, and file, always current because email/calendar are synced. Views are infinite and personal-until-saved.
Twenty/Folk go Notion-flavored: inline-editable everything, no edit-mode round trips, minimal chrome, records as flexible documents rather than forms.
The explicit anti-pattern is Salesforce itself: dense static field sections, tab-partitioned everything, 200-entry raw activity logs that "contain complete history but communicate no narrative."
Progressive summarization on records: current relationship status derived from recent interactions shown at top; the raw log is available but not the default.
Relevance to us: we already have the hard part (Gmail/Calendar sync feeding a real timeline — that's Attio's moat feature). What we lack is the highlights strip — lifetime giving, last gift, open pipeline, connection status, owner, next task as scannable widgets at the top of donor records — and a derived "where this relationship stands" line above the activity feed (we have the AI plumbing for it). Inline editing we partially have; making it universal is the Twenty/Folk move.

My prioritized shortlist, highest value-to-effort for this app:

Donor-record highlights strip (Attio pattern) — biggest visible win, pure frontend over data we already compute.
Match-confidence + one-click confirm in reconciliation — folds naturally into the two-reports redesign rather than being separate work.
Relationship-status summary line on donor records — leverages existing email-intelligence plumbing.
Cmd-K command palette — cheap, instantly modernizes feel.
Broader calm-design pass (progressive disclosure on dense detail pages) — worthwhile but diffuse; best done opportunistically.
